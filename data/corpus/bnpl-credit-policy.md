# BNPL Credit Policy Adjustment Note

## Portfolio context

The BNPL book showed a persistent rise in 30-plus day delinquency among first-time borrowers in discretionary retail. The increase was not uniform across the portfolio; it was concentrated in longer-duration plans, thinner-income files, and borrowers entering through high-incentive acquisition channels.

## Policy changes after delinquency increased

After 30-plus day delinquency increased, the credit team raised minimum down payment for higher-risk bands, shortened the maximum tenor on thin-file segments, and added targeted income verification for applicants with unstable repayment signals. The team also lowered approval intensity in acquisition channels that produced fast volume but weak first-payment performance. These changes were meant to reduce loss slope rather than maximize short-term origination.

## Why the policy was targeted

The team avoided a portfolio-wide tightening because broad tightening would have reduced healthy repeat-user volume along with the problem segments. A targeted response preserves revenue quality while attacking the specific combinations of tenor, acquisition source, and thin-file behavior that were driving 30-plus delinquency.

## Monitoring

BNPL policy changes are monitored through first-payment default, 7-day grace cure rate, and 30-plus day delinquency by vintage. Vintage tracking matters because policy improvements often look flat in blended portfolio numbers even when new approvals are performing better.

## Coordination with finance

Finance wants to know whether delinquency pressure is cyclical, underwriting-driven, or channel-driven. Credit policy therefore publishes both operational metrics and a short causal note so downstream teams can decide whether the issue belongs in pricing, acquisition, or risk appetite.
